Benjamin Harrison

23rd President of the United States

Early Life & Education

Born on August 20, 1833, in North Bend, Ohio, Benjamin Harrison came from a distinguished political family - he was the grandson of President William Henry Harrison. He graduated from Miami University in Oxford, Ohio, in 1852.

After studying law, Harrison moved to Indianapolis to establish his practice. His leadership qualities emerged during the Civil War, where he served as a colonel in the Union Army, an experience that greatly influenced his future political perspectives.

Presidency (1889-1893)

As a member of the Republican Party, Harrison won the 1888 election against incumbent Grover Cleveland, despite losing the popular vote. His presidency was marked by several significant achievements:

  • Signed the Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)
  • Created national forest reserves
  • Expanded the U.S. Navy
  • Admitted six new states to the Union

Legacy

After his presidency, Harrison returned to his law practice and became an active public speaker. He notably represented Venezuela in a boundary dispute with the United Kingdom. Harrison passed away from pneumonia on March 13, 1901, in Indianapolis, Indiana.
